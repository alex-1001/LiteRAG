# Harvesting and Processing Honey

Honey is ready to harvest when bees cap most cells in a frame—meaning moisture content is typically below 18%.

## When to Harvest

In temperate regions, the main harvest often occurs in late summer after the primary nectar flow. Leave at least 60–80 pounds of honey on the hive for winter in cold climates (roughly one full deep box plus partial stores).

## Removing Supers

Beekeepers use one or more of:

- **Bee escape**: A one-way valve board left on overnight
- **Fume board**: Brief use of a non-residual repellent
- **Brush or blower**: Manual removal at the hive

Never harvest brood boxes unless performing a specific management plan—those frames contain larvae and pollen.

## Extraction Steps

1. Uncap frames with a hot knife or capping scratcher.
2. Spin frames in a radial or tangential extractor.
3. Strain honey through a coarse filter to remove wax bits.
4. Let honey settle 24–48 hours, then bottle.

Raw honey crystallizes faster when stored below 50°F. Gentle warming to 100–110°F reliquifies without damaging enzymes.

## Yield Expectations

A strong colony on good forage might fill two medium supers (roughly 40–50 pounds extractable honey). Weak colonies or drought years may yield nothing surplus.

## Labeling Reminder

Many jurisdictions require net weight, producer name, and address on retail honey labels. "Wildflower" is a common floral source designation when specific crops are unknown.
